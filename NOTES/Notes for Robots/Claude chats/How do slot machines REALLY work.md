You're actually thinking about this in a pretty healthy way – treating it as entertainment with a fixed budget you're willing to lose, rather than as income generation. Let me break down how these machines actually work:

## The Math Behind Slot Machines

Electronic slot machines use something called RTP (Return to Player), typically ranging from 85-98% depending on the casino and jurisdiction. So if a machine has 92% RTP, over millions of spins, it'll pay back $92 for every $100 wagered. That missing 8% is the house edge – that's their profit margin.

The key thing: this plays out over _millions_ of spins. On any given night, variance means you could hit way above or below that average. That's why some nights you'll turn $20 into $100, and other nights it vanishes in 10 minutes.

## Can You "Game" This?

Not really, in the way you're hoping:

- **No skill element**: Unlike poker or blackjack, there's zero decision-making. You press a button, RNG (random number generator) determines the outcome instantly
- **Each spin is independent**: Previous results don't influence future ones
- **The math always wins long-term**: Play enough sessions and you'll trend toward that RTP percentage

The closest thing to "strategy" is:

- Playing higher RTP machines if you know which ones they are (casinos rarely advertise this)
- Slower play = entertainment lasts longer for your budget
- Avoiding progressive jackpots if you want steadier small wins (they have worse base RTP)

## Your "Entertainment Budget" Approach

This is actually the _only_ mathematically honest way to approach slots. You're essentially paying for the experience – the lights, sounds, occasional dopamine hit of a win. If you budget $40 and sometimes walk out with $80 to spend on dinner, that's a fun night. But you need to genuinely be okay with that $40 evaporating, because most nights it will.

The people calling it a "second job" are deep in gambler's fallacy territory – they're either ignoring their losses or in serious trouble.

## The Actual Trap

The 85-98% RTP sounds _almost_ fair, right? But here's the insidious part:

**Speed kills your bankroll.** Electronic slots can run 600-1200 spins per hour. Even at 92% RTP, if you're betting $1/spin at 600 spins/hour, you're cycling through $600 in wagers. That 8% house edge means you're expected to _lose_ $48/hour on average. Your $50 doesn't last long at all.

The "second job" logic you described – the desperation play where you need $300 for groceries and only have $50 – that's exactly the vulnerability these machines exploit. Mathematically, you're way more likely to turn that $50 into $0 than into $300. But when you're desperate and out of options, even a 5% shot at solving your problem feels worth taking.

## Why It's So Common in Poor Areas

You're right that it's not quite crack-dealer level extraction, but it's still wealth transfer from poor communities:

- The math guarantees steady losses for players, steady profits for operators
- It's designed to give _just enough_ small wins to keep people playing ("see, it does pay out!")
- The occasional big winner becomes the story everyone remembers, ignoring the hundreds of losers

The difference from your initial mental model: instead of instantly vaporizing money, it creates this slow drip of false hope. You lose $40, but you had a "near miss" on a big payout, so you come back. That's arguably _more_ psychologically addictive than just straight losing.
